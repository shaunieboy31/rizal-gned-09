José Protasio Rizal Mercado y Alonso Realonda.

"The pen is mightier than the sword"

Introduction

was a Filipino nationalist, writer and polymath active at the end of the Spanish colonial period of the Philippines. He is considered a national hero (pambansang bayani) of the Philippines.An ophthalmologist by profession, Rizal became a writer and a key member of the Filipino Propaganda Movement, which advocated political reforms for the colony under Spain. He was executed by the Spanish colonial government for the crime of rebellion after the Philippine Revolution broke out; it was inspired by his writings. Though he was not actively involved in its planning or conduct, he ultimately approved of its goals which eventually resulted in Philippine independence.

Biography

Life of José Rizal

Early Life and Education

José Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, to a wealthy family. He was the seventh of eleven children born to Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso. A gifted child, Rizal learned the alphabet by age 3 and could read and write by age 5.

Education and Travels

He studied at Ateneo Municipal de Manila and later took up medicine at the University of Santo Tomas. Dissatisfied, he continued his studies in Madrid, where he earned a degree in ophthalmology. His travels across Europe broadened his outlook and inspired him to write against colonial injustices.

Legacy

Rizal’s life was a testament to peaceful reform. His novels, essays, and martyrdom became the spark that ignited the Philippine revolution. He was executed on December 30, 1896, at Bagumbayan (now Luneta), leaving behind a legacy of courage, education, and patriotism.

José Rizal Works

Noli Me Tángere

The plot revolves around Crisostomo Ibarra, mixed-race heir of a wealthy clan, returning home after seven years in Europe and filled with ideas on how to better the lot of his countrymen

El Filibusterismo

El filibusterismo, also known by its alternative English title The Reign of Greed, is the second novel written by Philippine national hero José Rizal.

To the Young Women of Malolos

In February 1889, Rizal wrote a letter to the young women of Malolos in response to their petition to Governor-General Weyler to open a night school for women.

Mi Ultimo Adios

was Rizal's poetic tribute, his last farewell to the country he loved so much. Rizal died without seeing the dawn of a new day, yet he did see the downing of a new democracy in our country.

Goodbye to Leonor

It tells the story of Rizal's farewell to his childhood sweetheart, Leonor Rivera, before he left for Europe to study. The poem is filled with emotion and longing, as Rizal expresses his love for Leonor and his sadness at having to leave her behind.

Rizal Museum in Dapitan

The Rizal Museum in Dapitan, Zamboanga del Norte, is a historical landmark located within the José Rizal Memorial Protected Landscape. This 439-hectare site served as the place of exile for Dr. José Rizal from 1892 to 1896. Today, it stands as a tribute to his legacy and contributions to the Filipino people. The museum complex features reconstructed structures such as Rizal’s main residence, known as Casa Residencia, along with Casa Redonda (his clinic and student dormitory), Casa Cuadrada (a workshop and additional dormitory), and the Casitas de Salud (huts for male and female patients). Other notable installations include a kitchen, henhouse, dam, aqueduct, and an amphitheater, showcasing Rizal’s ingenuity in medicine, architecture, and community development. At the heart of the complex is the Rizaliana Museum, built in 1971 and renovated in 2016, which houses a collection of Rizal’s personal belongings, letters, clothing, and teaching materials. The museum is divided into four thematic galleries: “Exile to Dapitan,” “Rizal’s Scientific and Artistic Pursuits,” “Memories of Dapitan,” and “Road to Martyrdom.” These galleries provide an in-depth look at Rizal’s intellectual and humanitarian activities during his exile. The site also includes the iconic Mi Retiro Rock by the shoreline, a quiet spot where Rizal wrote his poem “Mi Retiro” and spent time with Josephine Bracken. Surrounded by lush greenery and overlooking the serene Dapitan Bay, the museum and its surroundings offer visitors a peaceful and reflective experience. It highlights Rizal not only as a national hero but also as a doctor, teacher, artist, scientist, and community leader. The Rizal Museum in Dapitan is open from Tuesday to Sunday, 8:00 AM to 4:00 PM, and serves as a powerful reminder of Rizal’s enduring influence and dedication to the betterment of the Filipino people.
