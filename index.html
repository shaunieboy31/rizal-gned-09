"The pen is mightier than the sword"

Introduction

was a Filipino nationalist, writer and polymath active at the end of the Spanish colonial period of the Philippines. He is considered a national hero (pambansang bayani) of the Philippines.An ophthalmologist by profession, Rizal became a writer and a key member of the Filipino Propaganda Movement, which advocated political reforms for the colony under Spain. He was executed by the Spanish colonial government for the crime of rebellion after the Philippine Revolution broke out; it was inspired by his writings. Though he was not actively involved in its planning or conduct, he ultimately approved of its goals which eventually resulted in Philippine independence.

Biography

Life of José Rizal

Early Life and Education

José Rizal was born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, to a wealthy family. He was the seventh of eleven children born to Francisco Mercado and Teodora Alonso. A gifted child, Rizal learned the alphabet by age 3 and could read and write by age 5.

Education and Travels

He studied at Ateneo Municipal de Manila and later took up medicine at the University of Santo Tomas. Dissatisfied, he continued his studies in Madrid, where he earned a degree in ophthalmology. His travels across Europe broadened his outlook and inspired him to write against colonial injustices.

Legacy

Rizal’s life was a testament to peaceful reform. His novels, essays, and martyrdom became the spark that ignited the Philippine revolution. He was executed on December 30, 1896, at Bagumbayan (now Luneta), leaving behind a legacy of courage, education, and patriotism.

Noli Me Tángere

El Filibusterismo

El filibusterismo, also known by its alternative English title The Reign of Greed, is the second novel written by Philippine national hero José Rizal.

To the Young Women of Malolos

In February 1889, Rizal wrote a letter to the young women of Malolos in response to their petition to Governor-General Weyler to open a night school for women.

Mi Ultimo Adios

was Rizal's poetic tribute, his last farewell to the country he loved so much. Rizal died without seeing the dawn of a new day, yet he did see the downing of a new democracy in our country.

Goodbye to Leonor

It tells the story of Rizal's farewell to his childhood sweetheart, Leonor Rivera, before he left for Europe to study. The poem is filled with emotion and longing, as Rizal expresses his love for Leonor and his sadness at having to leave her behind.

Fort Santiago

Fort Santiago is a historic citadel located in Intramuros, Manila. It served as a defense fortress during the Spanish colonial period and is famously known as the prison cell of Dr. José Rizal before his execution in 1896.

Rizal Museum in Dapitan

The Rizal Museum in Dapitan, Zamboanga del Norte, is part of the José Rizal Memorial Protected Landscape, where Dr. José Rizal spent his exile from 1892 to 1896. The site features replicas of Rizal’s home, clinic, dormitories, and other structures he built, reflecting his life as a teacher, doctor, and community leader. The Rizaliana Museum displays his personal items, letters, and memorabilia, while the nearby Mi Retiro Rock marks a peaceful spot where he wrote poetry. Surrounded by nature, the museum offers a glimpse into Rizal’s meaningful years in Dapitan and his lasting legacy.

Luneta Park

Luneta Park, also known as Rizal Park, is a historic landmark in Manila where Dr. José Rizal was executed in 1896. His remains lie beneath the iconic Rizal Monument, a symbol of Filipino heroism and freedom. The park features gardens, fountains, museums, and open spaces for leisure and national events, making it a place of reflection, history, and pride.

Rizal Shrine in Calamba

The Rizal Shrine in Calamba, Laguna, is a replica of the original house where Dr. José Rizal was born on June 19, 1861. Rebuilt in 1950, the two-story bahay na bato features antique furnishings, Rizal’s early writings, and family memorabilia. The grounds include a statue of young Rizal, a replica nipa hut, and the resting place of his parents. This national shrine offers visitors a glimpse into the hero’s childhood and the simple life that shaped his values.

José Rizal Monument in Florida

Located in Lake Eola Park, Orlando, the José Rizal Monument is a life-size bronze bust honoring the Filipino national hero. Installed in 2001 by the Filipino-American community, it celebrates Rizal’s legacy as a writer, reformist, and patriot. The monument stands as a symbol of Filipino pride and unity in Central Florida.

Jose Rizal Monument in Japan

In a quiet corner of Hibiya Park, central Tokyo—just steps from the former Tokyo Hotel where Rizal stayed in 1888—stands a bronze bust of Dr. José Rizal, mounted on a stone plaque that bears his name and historical details. The original 1961 marker was enhanced in 1998, the centennial of Philippine independence, with the addition of the bust. The monument is maintained by the Philippine Embassy and local organizations, and is honored annually with wreath-laying ceremonies on Rizal Day and his birthday

Jose Rizal Monument in Singapore

In the heart of Singapore’s Civic District, near the iconic Asian Civilisations Museum and Anderson Bridge along the Singapore River, stands a Jose Rizal historic marker unveiled in 2008. This double‑sided bronze plaque features a portrait relief of Rizal by Guillermo Tolentino (via Peter de Guzman) on one side, and a painting reproduction by Fabián de la Rosa on the other. It commemorates Rizal’s multiple visits to Singapore between 1882 and 1896 and is part of the National Heritage Board’s “Friends to Our Shores” series .

José Rizal Monument in Texas

The Dr. José P. Rizal Monument in Houston, Texas, is a bronze bust located at Hermann Park’s McGovern Centennial Gardens. Installed in 2006 and sculpted by Lorena Toritch, the monument honors Rizal’s legacy as the national hero of the Philippines—a writer, doctor, and reformist whose martyrdom inspired the fight for independence. The bust is mounted on a pedestal inscribed with one of Rizal’s famous quotes: “It is a useless life that is not consecrated to a great ideal...” Surrounded by a peaceful garden setting, the monument serves as a tribute to Rizal’s ideals of peace, education, and national pride, connecting Filipino heritage with global appreciation.

Paco Park in Manila

Paco Park in Manila is a historic cemetery where Dr. José Rizal was secretly buried after his execution on December 30, 1896. A simple marker now stands at the site of his original grave, honoring his sacrifice for the nation. Surrounded by serene gardens and old stone walls, Paco Park remains a peaceful tribute to the hero’s memory and a quiet place for reflection.

Rizal Monument in Alaska

In downtown Juneau’s Manila Square stands a bust of Dr. José Rizal atop a stone pedestal. Unveiled in 2002, the bronze sculpture honors Rizal’s legacy and commemorates the long history of Filipino-Alaskan ties, from early telegraph work to contributions in canneries and mining
